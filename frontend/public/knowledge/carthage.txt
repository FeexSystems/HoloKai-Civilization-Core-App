CARTHAGE (NORTH AFRICA)

Carthage was a major Phoenician-founded power on the North African coast (near modern Tunis, Tunisia). From the 9th/8th century BCE until its destruction by Rome in 146 BCE, Carthage dominated western Mediterranean trade and contested Rome in the Punic Wars.

KEY ACHIEVEMENTS:
- Extensive maritime trade network across North Africa, Iberia, Sicily, Sardinia, and beyond
- Advanced harbor engineering (cothon) and shipbuilding
- Agricultural innovation in the Maghreb; agronomic knowledge later transmitted via Roman sources (e.g., Mago)
- Military and diplomatic reach under leaders such as Hamilcar Barca and Hannibal Barca
- Urban complexity: temples, markets, residential quarters, and industrial zones
- Syncretic religion (Baal Hammon, Tanit) and Punic language culture

AFRICAN CONTEXT:
- Carthage was an African-based Mediterranean empire drawing resources and recruits from the Maghreb and Sahara margins
- Interaction with Numidian and other North African polities shaped regional politics
- Punic and later Romano-African culture left deep marks on North African urban and agricultural history
- After 146 BCE, Roman Africa Proconsularis rose on Carthaginian foundations; a Roman colony later refounded Carthage

KEY FIGURES & EPISODES:
- Hannibal Barca — crossed the Alps and defeated Rome at Cannae (216 BCE)
- Queen Dido (Elissa) — legendary founder in classical tradition
- Third Punic War — Roman siege and destruction of the city (146 BCE)

LEGACY:
- Carthage is essential to understanding African participation in classical Mediterranean history
- Numidian successors and Romano-African provinces continued North Africa's role as a grain and intellectual center
- Modern Tunisian archaeology (Byrsa, ports, tophet debates) continually revises Punic studies
