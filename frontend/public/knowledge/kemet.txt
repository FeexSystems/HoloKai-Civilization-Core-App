KEMET (ANCIENT EGYPT)

Kemet, meaning "the Black Land" in ancient Egyptian, was one of the earliest and most enduring civilizations in human history. Located along the Nile River in Northeast Africa, Kemet flourished for over 3,000 years (c. 3100 BCE – 332 BCE).

KEY ACHIEVEMENTS:
- One of the first centralized governments with a divine kingship (Pharaoh) system
- Advanced mathematical knowledge including geometry, fractions, and the concept of pi
- Sophisticated medical practices including surgery, dentistry, and the Edwin Smith Papyrus (c. 1600 BCE)
- Monumental architecture: the Great Pyramid of Giza (c. 2560 BCE), temples of Karnak and Luxor
- The invention of written language (hieroglyphs) — one of the earliest writing systems
- The concept of Ma'at: truth, balance, order, law, morality, and justice
- Advanced astronomical observations and the 365-day solar calendar
- Papyrus manufacturing and extensive record-keeping

CULTURAL SIGNIFICANCE:
- Kemet was a Black African civilization, a fact acknowledged by early historians including Herodotus
- Its knowledge systems influenced Greek, Roman, and later Western civilizations
- The Library of Alexandria preserved vast amounts of Kemetian knowledge
- Kemetic spirituality and philosophy deeply influenced later African cosmologies

KEY FIGURES:
- Pharaoh Khufu (Cheops) — builder of the Great Pyramid
- Pharaoh Hatshepsut — one of history's first great female rulers
- Pharaoh Ramesses II — prolific builder and military leader
- Akhenaten — introduced monotheistic worship of Aten
- Imhotep — architect, physician, and polymath, later deified
